# Sinusitis Treatment Guide: Understanding Sinus Infections and Finding Relief

Sinusitis, commonly known as a sinus infection, is one of the most prevalent conditions treated by ENT specialists worldwide — and Uganda is no exception. Affecting millions of people each year, sinusitis can range from a mild inconvenience lasting a few days to a debilitating chronic condition that significantly impacts quality of life. This guide covers what sinusitis is, what causes it, how to recognise it, and what treatment options are available.

## What Is Sinusitis?

Your sinuses are hollow, air-filled cavities located within the bones of your face and skull. There are four pairs: the maxillary sinuses (behind the cheeks), the ethmoid sinuses (between the eyes), the frontal sinuses (above the eyebrows), and the sphenoid sinuses (behind the nose). These cavities are lined with a thin mucous membrane that filters air and drains through small openings into the nasal passages.

Sinusitis occurs when this lining becomes inflamed and swollen — usually due to infection, allergy, or another irritant — causing the drainage openings to become blocked. Mucus then accumulates inside the sinuses, creating an environment where bacteria, viruses, or fungi can thrive.

## Types of Sinusitis

Understanding which type of sinusitis you have guides the right treatment approach.

**Acute Sinusitis:**
- Lasts up to 4 weeks
- Usually follows a cold or upper respiratory infection
- Most commonly caused by viruses
- Symptoms come on suddenly and are often severe

**Subacute Sinusitis:**
- Lasts 4 to 12 weeks
- May represent a transition between acute and chronic forms
- Often bacterial in origin

**Chronic Sinusitis:**
- Persists for 12 weeks or longer despite treatment attempts
- May involve structural problems, polyps, or ongoing allergic inflammation
- Requires specialist ENT evaluation

**Recurrent Acute Sinusitis:**
- Four or more acute episodes per year
- Each episode resolves completely between attacks
- Warrants investigation for underlying causes

## Common Causes of Sinusitis

### Infections
**Viral infections** are the most frequent trigger, typically following a common cold. The nasal congestion caused by the virus swells the sinus openings and impairs drainage.

**Bacterial infections** often develop secondary to a viral episode. Key bacterial species include:
- *Streptococcus pneumoniae*
- *Haemophilus influenzae*
- *Moraxella catarrhalis*

**Fungal sinusitis** is less common but occurs more frequently in individuals with weakened immune systems or uncontrolled diabetes.

### Non-Infectious Triggers
- **Allergic rhinitis** — ongoing nasal inflammation from dust, pollen, or mould keeps sinus drainage impaired
- **Nasal polyps** — soft tissue growths that block sinus openings
- **Deviated nasal septum** — structural asymmetry that impairs drainage on one side
- **Dental infections** — upper molar roots sit close to the maxillary sinus floor and can spread infection
- **Air pollution and smoke** — common triggers in urban Uganda

## Recognising Sinusitis: Key Symptoms

### Main Symptoms
- **Facial pain or pressure** — typically felt over the cheeks, forehead, or between the eyes; worsens when bending forward
- **Nasal congestion** — blocked nose that makes breathing through the nose difficult
- **Thick nasal discharge** — yellow or green mucus draining from the nose or down the throat (post-nasal drip)
- **Reduced sense of smell** — often one of the first symptoms patients notice
- **Headache** — particularly in the morning or after stooping

### Additional Symptoms
- Fatigue and general malaise
- Sore throat and cough from post-nasal drip
- Bad breath (halitosis)
- Ear fullness or mild hearing changes
- Fever (more common in acute bacterial sinusitis)

### Warning Signs That Need Urgent Attention
Seek immediate medical care if you experience:
- Severe headache that is the worst of your life
- Stiff neck or sensitivity to light
- Swelling around one or both eyes
- Visual changes or double vision
- Confusion or altered consciousness
- High fever above 39°C that does not improve

These symptoms can indicate serious complications such as orbital cellulitis or meningitis.

## Diagnosis

An ENT specialist will typically assess sinusitis through:

**Clinical history and examination:** Assessing symptom duration, severity, and character, combined with nasal examination using a headlight or speculum.

**Nasal endoscopy:** A thin flexible camera passed through the nostril allows direct visualisation of the sinus openings, polyps, and discharge. This is the gold standard for chronic sinusitis assessment.

**Imaging:** CT scanning of the sinuses provides detailed information about the extent of disease, anatomy, and any structural abnormalities — essential before considering surgery.

**Nasal swab/culture:** Identifies the causative organism and guides antibiotic selection in refractory cases.

## Treatment Options

### Acute Sinusitis Management

Most acute viral sinusitis resolves within 10 days with supportive care:

- **Saline nasal irrigation** (e.g., a neti pot or squeeze bottle) — flushes mucus and irritants, reduces swelling
- **Nasal decongestant sprays** — for short-term use (maximum 3–5 days to avoid rebound congestion)
- **Steam inhalation** — provides temporary relief of congestion
- **Adequate hydration** — helps thin mucus for easier drainage
- **Pain relief** — paracetamol or ibuprofen for facial pain and fever

**Antibiotics** are only appropriate when symptoms are clearly bacterial (purulent discharge, facial pain, fever) and persist beyond 10 days, or worsen after initial improvement. Overuse of antibiotics contributes to resistance and should be avoided.

### Chronic Sinusitis Management

Chronic sinusitis requires a more structured approach:

**Medical management:**
- Intranasal corticosteroid sprays (e.g., fluticasone, budesonide) — reduce mucosal inflammation over weeks to months
- Saline irrigation — daily use to maintain sinus hygiene
- Treatment of underlying allergy — antihistamines, allergy testing, and immunotherapy where indicated
- Short courses of oral corticosteroids in severe flare-ups

**Surgical management — Functional Endoscopic Sinus Surgery (FESS):**
When medical treatment fails, FESS is the standard surgical option. Using an endoscope, the surgeon widens the natural sinus openings, removes polyps, and corrects structural obstructions — all without external incisions. Recovery is typically 1–2 weeks.

## Prevention

- Keep nasal passages moist — use saline sprays in dry or dusty environments
- Manage allergies proactively before they trigger sinus inflammation
- Avoid tobacco smoke and air pollutants where possible
- Treat colds early with adequate rest and hydration
- Stay up to date with vaccinations to reduce respiratory infection risk

## When to See an ENT Specialist

Consult an ENT specialist if:
- Symptoms last more than 10 days without improvement
- You have had more than three sinus infections in a year
- You have nasal polyps or a deviated septum
- Symptoms are not responding to standard treatment
- You develop any of the warning signs listed above
