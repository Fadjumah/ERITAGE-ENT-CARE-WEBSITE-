# Submandibular Lump Sensation: Why You Feel a Lump Under Your Jaw When Nothing Is Visible

Many patients visit our clinic in Entebbe with a puzzling complaint: they feel a distinct lump or fullness under their jaw, yet when they look in the mirror or when a doctor examines them, nothing appears swollen. This "phantom lump" or sensation of fullness is more common than you might think. At **Eritage ENT Care-Entebbe**, we frequently diagnose and treat the various underlying causes of this discomfort.

## What is a Submandibular Lump Sensation?

The submandibular region is the area beneath your lower jaw. It contains salivary glands, lymph nodes, muscles, and nerves. A "sensation" of a lump occurs when you feel pressure or fullness even if no structural mass is visible to the eye.

## Common Causes of Feeling a Lump Under the Jaw

### 1. Globus Pharyngeus (Throat Tightness)
Globus is the sensation of a lump or tightness in the throat or under the jaw, often triggered by stress or [Laryngopharyngeal Reflux (LPR)](/blog/throat-pain-swallowing-difficulty). It characteristically improves when eating or drinking.

### 2. Salivary Gland Issues
The submandibular glands produce most of your saliva. Sensation of fullness can be caused by:
- **Salivary Stones**: Small calcium deposits blocking a duct.
- **Infection (Sialadenitis)**: Often causing pain that worsens during meals.

### 3. Reactive Lymph Nodes
Lymph nodes under the jaw frequently enlarge in response to nearby issues like:
- **Dental Infections**: Such as a tooth abscess or gum disease.
- **Tonsillitis**: [Inflammation of the tonsils](/conditions/tonsillitis-entebbe) often causes tender nodes.
- **Mouth Ulcers**: Common in Uganda's seasonal weather changes.

### 4. Muscle Tension
Stress or poor posture can cause the muscles in the floor of the mouth to become tense and painful, mimicking the feeling of a lump.

## When Should You Be Concerned About a Neck Lump?

While most sensations are benign, you should seek professional [ENT evaluation in Entebbe](/ent-services) immediately if you notice:
- A lump that has been present for **more than 3 weeks**.
- A lump that is **hard, firm, or fixed** in place.
- A **painless** lump that is steadily growing in size.
- Associated symptoms like unexplained weight loss, night sweats, or [difficulty swallowing](/blog/throat-pain-swallowing-difficulty).
