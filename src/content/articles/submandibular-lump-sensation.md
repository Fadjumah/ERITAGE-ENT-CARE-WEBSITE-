# Submandibular Lump Sensation: Why You Feel a Lump Under Your Jaw When Nothing Is Visible

Many patients visit ENT clinics with a puzzling complaint: they feel a distinct lump or fullness under their jaw, yet when they look in the mirror or when a doctor examines them, nothing appears swollen. This frustrating experience is more common than you might think — and in most cases, it has a recognisable explanation. This guide explores the anatomy of the submandibular region, the most common causes of this sensation, and the important distinction between a sensation of fullness and a true structural lump that requires investigation.

## Anatomy of the Submandibular Region

The submandibular triangle is the area beneath the lower jaw (mandible) on either side. This region contains several important structures:

- **Submandibular salivary glands** — a pair of walnut-sized glands that produce approximately 70% of the saliva in your mouth
- **Lymph nodes** — a cluster of 3–6 lymph nodes in each submandibular triangle that drain the teeth, gums, lips, floor of mouth, and parts of the tongue
- **Muscles** — the mylohyoid and digastric muscles form the floor of the mouth
- **Nerves and blood vessels** — including the lingual nerve and facial artery

Because this area is rich in structures that respond to inflammation, infection, or altered function, it is a common site of discomfort and perceived swelling.

## Common Causes of a Submandibular Lump Sensation

### 1. Globus Pharyngeus (Globus Sensation)

Globus pharyngeus — often simply called globus — is the sensation of a lump, tightness, or foreign body in the throat, typically felt at the level of the larynx or just below it. Some patients localise it more to the submandibular region.

It is one of the most common presentations to ENT outpatient clinics worldwide, accounting for around 4% of referrals.

**Why does it happen?**
The exact mechanism is debated, but contributing factors include:
- **Laryngopharyngeal reflux (LPR)** — acid or pepsin from the stomach reaches the throat, causing chronic low-grade irritation
- **Upper oesophageal sphincter dysfunction** — increased pressure or abnormal contractions of the muscle at the top of the oesophagus
- **Muscle tension** — stress, anxiety, or habitual throat-clearing cause the muscles of the pharynx and larynx to tighten
- **Post-nasal drip** — mucus dripping down the throat creates a constant awareness of the throat

Importantly, globus sensation is characteristically present between swallows and often improves or disappears when eating or drinking — the opposite of true dysphagia (difficulty swallowing), where swallowing is the problem.

**Management:**
- Dietary and lifestyle modifications to reduce acid reflux (avoid caffeine, spicy foods, alcohol, large meals before bed)
- Proton pump inhibitors (anti-reflux medication) if LPR is suspected
- Stress management and relaxation techniques
- Speech and language therapy exercises for pharyngeal muscle tension

### 2. Submandibular Salivary Gland Swelling

The submandibular glands can swell for several reasons:

**Salivary gland stones (sialolithiasis):**
Calcium deposits form within the gland's duct, partially or completely blocking saliva flow. This causes:
- Swelling under the jaw that characteristically appears during meals (when saliva production is stimulated) and subsides afterwards
- Pain or discomfort under the jaw when eating
- Sometimes a palpable tender lump

This is a common and treatable condition. Small stones can sometimes be massaged out; larger stones may require endoscopic or surgical removal.

**Salivary gland infection (sialadenitis):**
When a stone or duct narrowing blocks saliva flow, bacteria can ascend into the gland causing acute infection. Symptoms include:
- Tender, firm swelling under the jaw
- Redness and warmth of the overlying skin
- Purulent discharge from the duct opening inside the mouth
- Fever and generally feeling unwell

Treatment involves antibiotics, hydration, warm compresses, and gland massage to encourage drainage. Recurrent sialadenitis may eventually warrant surgical removal of the gland.

**Viral infection:**
Mumps (paramyxovirus) causes bilateral swelling of the salivary glands, though the parotid glands (in front of the ears) are more prominently affected. It is preventable with the MMR vaccine.

### 3. Lymph Node Enlargement (Lymphadenopathy)

The submandibular lymph nodes enlarge in response to infections or inflammation in their drainage territory. Common causes include:

- Dental infections or abscesses — the most common cause of submandibular lymphadenopathy in Uganda and across Africa
- Gum disease (periodontitis)
- [Tonsillitis or pharyngitis](/blog/tonsillitis-causes-symptoms)
- Mouth ulcers or infections of the lips
- Skin infections of the lower face

Reactive lymph nodes from infection are typically:
- Mildly to moderately enlarged (1–2 cm)
- Soft and mobile
- Tender to touch
- Accompanied by an obvious nearby infection

They almost always resolve as the underlying infection clears, usually within 2–4 weeks.

### 4. Dental and Periodontal Disease

An infected tooth or abscess — particularly from lower back molars — can cause significant pain and fullness radiating into the submandibular region. The sensation of swelling may precede any visible facial swelling. Dental infections can also cause reactive lymph node enlargement in the submandibular triangle.

Assessment by a dentist alongside ENT evaluation is often appropriate when submandibular symptoms are present.

### 5. Muscle Tension and Myofascial Pain

The muscles of the floor of the mouth and neck can become tense and painful due to:
- Stress and anxiety
- Poor posture (particularly common with prolonged mobile phone or computer use)
- Bruxism (grinding teeth at night)
- Temporomandibular joint (TMJ) dysfunction

Myofascial pain in these muscles can produce a deep aching sensation under the jaw that is easily mistaken for a lump, particularly as the muscles can feel firm and nodular on palpation. Physiotherapy, massage, and addressing the underlying cause (stress, posture, TMJ) typically help.

### 6. Thyroid and Parathyroid Conditions

The thyroid gland lies lower in the neck, but thyroid enlargement or nodules can occasionally produce a sense of fullness that patients describe as being "under the jaw." Similarly, parathyroid conditions can cause vague neck and jaw sensations. A thyroid examination and, if indicated, ultrasound will clarify.

## When Is a Submandibular Lump Truly Concerning?

The following features in a submandibular lump or sensation warrant prompt ENT evaluation:

- **A lump that has been present for more than 3 weeks** without a clear resolving cause
- **A lump that is growing in size**
- **A hard, firm, or fixed lump** — unlike the soft, mobile feel of reactive lymph nodes
- **A painless lump** — reactive nodes from infection are usually tender; painless enlargement raises more concern
- **Associated symptoms:** unexplained weight loss, drenching night sweats, persistent fatigue, difficulty swallowing, or voice change
- **Skin changes** over the lump — redness, ulceration, or tethering
- **A lump in a person who smokes or drinks alcohol** — these are risk factors for head and neck cancer

These features do not confirm cancer — most neck lumps are benign — but they are reasons to seek evaluation promptly rather than waiting. Head and neck cancers detected at an early stage have significantly better outcomes.

## What to Expect at an ENT Consultation

An ENT specialist will:
- Take a detailed history of the sensation or lump — duration, changes over time, associated symptoms
- Perform a thorough head and neck examination, including palpation of all lymph node regions, the salivary glands, and thyroid
- Examine the mouth, throat, and post-nasal space — often with a flexible nasopharyngolaryngoscope to assess areas not visible to the naked eye
- Arrange investigations as appropriate: ultrasound scan of the neck (the most useful first imaging tool), fine needle aspiration biopsy (FNAB) of suspicious nodes, blood tests (including thyroid function and EBV serology), or CT/MRI if a deep or extensive mass is suspected

In the majority of cases, the assessment provides reassurance and a clear diagnosis. When further investigation is needed, it is organised promptly.

## Summary

A submandibular lump sensation is rarely sinister, but it deserves proper assessment. The most common causes — globus sensation, salivary gland issues, reactive lymph nodes from dental or throat infections, and muscle tension — all have straightforward treatments. However, a persistent, growing, hard, or painless lump always warrants ENT evaluation, and the earlier any genuine pathology is identified, the better the outcome.
