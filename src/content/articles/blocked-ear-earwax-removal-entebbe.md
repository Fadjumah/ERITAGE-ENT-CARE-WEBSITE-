A blocked ear in Entebbe is one of the most common complaints we see at Eritage ENT Care. The sensation of muffled hearing, fullness, or pressure in the ear is uncomfortable — and in many cases, entirely treatable in a single clinic visit.

This article explains what causes a blocked ear, what you can safely try at home, and when you must see an ENT specialist.

## What causes a blocked ear?

The ear can become blocked at several points along its anatomy. The most common causes are:

**Earwax buildup (cerumen impaction)**

Earwax is produced naturally to protect the ear canal. In some people, it accumulates faster than it can migrate out, hardening and pressing against the eardrum. This is the most common cause of a blocked ear in adults and children in Uganda.

**Fluid in the middle ear**

After an upper respiratory infection — a cold, flu, or sinusitis — fluid can become trapped behind the eardrum. This is called otitis media with effusion (or "glue ear" in children). It causes a feeling of fullness and reduced hearing.

**Eustachian tube dysfunction**

The Eustachian tube connects the middle ear to the back of the throat and regulates pressure. When it does not open and close properly — after flying, diving, or during a cold — the pressure difference causes a blocked sensation.

**Ear infection**

Both outer ear infections (otitis externa) and middle ear infections cause swelling, fluid, and sometimes debris that obstruct the canal or middle ear.

**Foreign body**

In children especially, small objects, seeds, insects, or cotton bud tips get lodged in the ear canal.

## Symptoms of a blocked ear in Entebbe

- Muffled or dull hearing, as if your ears need to "pop"
- A feeling of pressure or fullness inside the ear
- Your own voice sounding louder or echoing in your head
- Mild aching or discomfort
- Tinnitus (ringing or buzzing sounds)
- Dizziness or balance problems in some cases

## What can you safely try at home?

For **mild wax**, over-the-counter olive oil drops (3–5 drops, twice daily for up to 5 days) can soften the wax and allow it to migrate out naturally.

For **pressure after flying**, try the Valsalva manoeuvre: pinch your nose, close your mouth, and gently blow until your ears pop. Swallowing and yawning also help.

**Do not:**
- Insert cotton buds — they push wax deeper and risk perforating the eardrum
- Use ear candles — there is no evidence they work, and they cause burns
- Use any object to probe the ear canal

If the blockage does not resolve in 48–72 hours, see an ENT specialist.

## When to see an ENT specialist in Entebbe

Book an appointment at Eritage ENT Care if:

- The blockage has lasted more than 48 hours
- You have ear pain alongside the blockage
- There is any discharge from the ear
- You have a sudden reduction in hearing
- You feel dizzy or off-balance
- The blocked ear follows a head injury or a very loud sound
- The blockage is in a child under 2 years old

These symptoms suggest the cause may be more than simple wax, and home remedies are not appropriate.
