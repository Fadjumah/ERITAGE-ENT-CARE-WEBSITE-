# Snoring and Sleep Apnea: Causes, Risks, and Treatment Options

Snoring is often dismissed as a minor nuisance — something to joke about rather than address medically. But for millions of people, snoring is the first and most audible sign of a serious condition: obstructive sleep apnoea (OSA). At Eritage ENT Care, we help patients understand the difference between simple snoring and sleep apnoea, identify underlying causes, and access effective [snoring and sleep apnea treatments in Entebbe](/conditions/snoring-entebbe) that can transform both sleep quality and long-term health.

## What Causes Snoring?

Snoring occurs when airflow through the mouth and nose is partially blocked during sleep. As you relax into sleep, the muscles of the throat, tongue, and palate also relax. If the airway narrows sufficiently, passing air causes the surrounding soft tissues to vibrate — producing the characteristic snoring sound.

**Common anatomical contributors to snoring:**
- **Excess soft palate tissue** — a long or thick soft palate narrows the airway
- **Large tonsils or adenoids** — especially common in children
- **Enlarged uvula** — the small tissue hanging at the back of the throat
- **Excess throat tissue** — associated with overweight or obesity
- **Nasal obstruction** — a deviated septum, nasal polyps, or chronic congestion forces mouth breathing, which predisposes to snoring
- **Tongue position** — the tongue can fall backward during sleep, particularly in back-sleepers

**Lifestyle factors that worsen snoring:**
- Alcohol consumption — relaxes throat muscles further
- Sedative or sleeping tablets — same effect as alcohol
- Smoking — inflames and narrows the airway
- Sleeping on your back — allows the tongue and soft palate to collapse backward
- Sleep deprivation — deeper, more relaxed sleep increases snoring

## What Is Obstructive Sleep Apnoea?

Obstructive sleep apnoea (OSA) occurs when the upper airway collapses completely during sleep, causing breathing to stop repeatedly throughout the night. These episodes — called apnoeas — can last from 10 seconds to over a minute and may occur hundreds of times per night.

Each apnoea ends when the brain detects falling oxygen levels and sends an arousal signal, causing the person to rouse briefly (often without being aware of it) and restore the airway. This cycle of obstruction and arousal prevents restful, continuous sleep.

### Types of Sleep Apnoea
- **Obstructive sleep apnoea (OSA)** — the most common form; caused by physical collapse of the airway
- **Central sleep apnoea** — the brain fails to send the correct signals to the breathing muscles; less common
- **Mixed sleep apnoea** — a combination of both types

## Recognising the Symptoms

### Symptoms During Sleep (usually reported by a partner or family member):
- Loud, habitual snoring — often described as the loudest in the household
- Witnessed pauses in breathing, sometimes followed by a gasping or choking sound
- Restless, disturbed sleep with frequent position changes
- Sweating during sleep

### Daytime Symptoms (experienced by the patient):
- **Excessive daytime sleepiness** — falling asleep during conversations, meals, or while driving
- Waking unrefreshed despite a full night's sleep
- Morning headaches — caused by low oxygen and elevated carbon dioxide during sleep
- Difficulty concentrating, poor memory, or "brain fog"
- Irritability, mood changes, or depression
- Reduced libido
- Nocturia (waking repeatedly to urinate during the night)

### In Children:
- Loud snoring with pauses in breathing
- Mouth breathing and restless sleep
- Bedwetting beyond the expected age
- Hyperactivity, aggression, or difficulty concentrating (often mistaken for behavioural issues)
- Poor growth despite adequate nutrition

## The Health Risks of Untreated Sleep Apnoea

Untreated OSA is far more than a sleep inconvenience — it carries significant health risks:

**Cardiovascular disease:**
- **Hypertension** — OSA is found in up to 50% of people with high blood pressure
- **Heart arrhythmias** — particularly atrial fibrillation
- **Increased risk of heart attack and stroke** — repeated oxygen drops and surges in blood pressure strain the heart and blood vessels

**Metabolic effects:**
- Worsening of type 2 diabetes through insulin resistance
- Weight gain — sleep deprivation disrupts the hormones that regulate appetite
- Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease

**Neurological and psychological:**
- Depression and anxiety
- Cognitive decline with long-term untreated OSA
- Increased risk of motor vehicle accidents — drowsy driving is as dangerous as drunk driving

**In children:** Untreated OSA can impair growth hormone release (which occurs primarily during deep sleep), affect brain development, and contribute to learning difficulties.

## Who Is at Risk?

OSA can affect anyone, but certain factors significantly increase risk:

- **Overweight or obesity** — excess fatty tissue around the neck narrows the airway
- **Male sex** — men are 2–3 times more likely to have OSA than pre-menopausal women (risk equalises after menopause)
- **Increasing age** — muscle tone in the throat decreases with age
- **Family history** — craniofacial anatomy is partly genetic
- **Neck circumference** — greater than 40cm in women or 43cm in men is a risk factor
- **Nasal obstruction** — from any cause
- **Hypothyroidism** or acromegaly
- **Alcohol use** — particularly within 2–3 hours of bedtime

## Diagnosis

If OSA is suspected, diagnosis is confirmed by a sleep study:

**Home Sleep Apnoea Test (HSAT):** A portable device worn overnight at home measures oxygen saturation, heart rate, airflow, and respiratory effort. Convenient and suitable for most patients with a moderate-to-high pre-test probability of OSA.

**In-laboratory Polysomnography:** The gold standard sleep study, conducted in a sleep lab. Measures a comprehensive range of parameters including brain activity, eye movements, muscle tone, and limb movements in addition to breathing. Required for complex or equivocal cases.

Results are expressed as the **Apnoea-Hypopnoea Index (AHI):**
- AHI 5–14: Mild OSA
- AHI 15–29: Moderate OSA
- AHI ≥30: Severe OSA

An ENT specialist will assess the upper airway with nasopharyngolaryngoscopy to identify the anatomical sites contributing to obstruction — essential for planning treatment.

## Treatment Options

### Lifestyle Modifications (Mild OSA and as Adjuncts)
- **Weight loss** — even a 10% reduction in body weight can significantly reduce AHI
- **Sleep position** — avoiding sleeping on your back keeps the tongue forward
- **Reducing alcohol** — especially in the 3 hours before bed
- **Stopping smoking** — reduces airway inflammation
- **Regular physical activity** — improves sleep quality and airway muscle tone

### Continuous Positive Airway Pressure (CPAP)
CPAP is the most effective treatment for moderate-to-severe OSA. A machine delivers a constant stream of pressurised air through a mask worn over the nose or nose and mouth during sleep, acting as a pneumatic splint to keep the airway open.

Benefits of effective CPAP use:
- Eliminates snoring and apnoeas
- Restores restorative sleep
- Significantly reduces blood pressure and cardiovascular risk
- Improves mood, concentration, and daytime alertness

Adherence is the main challenge — modern CPAP machines are quieter and more comfortable than earlier models, and there are many mask styles to try.

### Mandibular Advancement Device (MAD)
A custom-fitted dental device that holds the lower jaw slightly forward during sleep, keeping the airway open. Effective for mild-to-moderate OSA, particularly in patients who cannot tolerate CPAP.

### Surgical Treatment
Surgery is considered when there is a correctable anatomical cause, or when CPAP is not tolerated.

**ENT surgical options include:**
- **Tonsillectomy and adenoidectomy** — highly effective in children; can be curative in adults when tonsils are the primary obstruction
- **Septoplasty** — straightening a deviated nasal septum to improve nasal airflow
- **Turbinate reduction** — reducing enlarged turbinate bones to open nasal passages
- **Uvulopalatopharyngoplasty (UPPP)** — removing excess soft palate tissue, the uvula, and tonsils to widen the pharyngeal airway
- **Tongue base surgery** — addressing tongue obstruction in selected patients
- **Hypoglossal nerve stimulation** — an implanted device that stimulates the tongue muscles during sleep (for patients with moderate-to-severe OSA who cannot tolerate CPAP)

## When to See an ENT Specialist for Snoring or Sleep Apnoea

Seek evaluation if:
- You are told you snore loudly or stop breathing during sleep
- You wake unrefreshed regardless of how long you sleep
- You are excessively sleepy during the day
- You have been diagnosed with hypertension, especially if difficult to control
- Your child snores regularly, breathes through the mouth, or shows behavioural or learning difficulties

Early diagnosis and treatment of OSA can prevent serious long-term health consequences and significantly improve your quality of life.
