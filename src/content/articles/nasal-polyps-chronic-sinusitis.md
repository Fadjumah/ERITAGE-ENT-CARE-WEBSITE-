# Nasal Polyps: Understanding, Managing & Treating This Common ENT Condition

Nasal polyps are soft, painless, noncancerous growths that develop in the lining of your nasal passages or sinuses. Often resulting from chronic inflammation due to rhinitis or sinusitis, these grape-like swellings can significantly impact your breathing, sense of smell, and overall quality of life. Despite being benign, nasal polyps are a common source of chronic suffering — and one that responds well to modern ENT treatment when properly managed. ## What Are Nasal Polyps?

Nasal polyps are benign outgrowths of the mucous membrane that lines the nasal cavity and paranasal sinuses. They are soft, mobile, and insensitive to touch — unlike nasal tumours, which are firm and may bleed. Polyps most commonly arise from the ethmoid sinuses (between the eyes) and grow downward into the nasal cavity, where they can obstruct airflow and sinus drainage.

They range in size from tiny, barely visible growths to large masses that can fill the entire nasal cavity. Most polyps occur on both sides of the nose (bilateral), which helps distinguish them from other nasal masses. Unilateral (one-sided) nasal polyps require more careful evaluation to exclude other pathology.

### Classification

**Ethmoidal polyps** (most common):
- Arise from the ethmoid sinuses
- Multiple, bilateral
- Associated with allergy, chronic sinusitis, and aspirin intolerance
- Tend to recur after treatment

**Antrochoanal polyps:**
- Arise from the lining of the maxillary sinus
- Usually single and unilateral
- More common in children and young adults
- Often present as a large polyp visible in the back of the nose or throat

## What Causes Nasal Polyps?

The exact mechanism triggering polyp formation is not fully understood, but it involves chronic inflammation of the nasal mucosa leading to oedema (fluid accumulation) and eventual polyp development.

### Associated Conditions

**Chronic rhinosinusitis (CRS):**
The most common underlying cause. Prolonged inflammation of the nasal and sinus lining — lasting 12 weeks or more — creates the inflammatory environment in which polyps form. CRS with nasal polyps (CRSwNP) is a recognised clinical entity.

**Allergic rhinitis:**
Persistent nasal allergy causes chronic mucosal inflammation. People with allergic rhinitis are significantly more likely to develop nasal polyps.

**Asthma:**
Approximately 20–30% of people with nasal polyps also have asthma. The nasal passages and bronchial tree share the same type of mucous membrane and are affected by similar inflammatory processes.

**Aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease (AERD):**
Also called Samter's triad — the combination of asthma, aspirin sensitivity, and nasal polyps. Patients develop worsening respiratory symptoms after taking aspirin or other NSAIDs.

**Non-allergic eosinophilic conditions:**
Some patients develop polyps without identifiable allergy — driven instead by an overactive immune response involving eosinophils (a type of white blood cell).

**Cystic fibrosis:**
Nasal polyps occur in up to 40% of patients with cystic fibrosis and may appear in early childhood.

**Fungal sinusitis:**
Allergic fungal sinusitis (AFS) is an increasingly recognised cause of polyps in tropical climates, including Uganda.

## Recognising Nasal Polyps: Symptoms

Nasal polyps are typically painless. Their symptoms arise from the physical obstruction they cause and the underlying inflammatory process.

### Common Symptoms
- **Persistent nasal obstruction** — blocked nose that does not resolve with decongestants
- **Reduced or absent sense of smell (hyposmia/anosmia)** — often the most troubling symptom and one of the earliest to appear
- **Reduced taste** — closely linked to loss of smell
- **Runny nose** — often with thick, discoloured mucus
- **Post-nasal drip** — mucus dripping down the back of the throat, causing throat clearing and cough
- **Facial pressure or fullness** — from blocked sinus drainage
- **Snoring** — from nasal obstruction forcing mouth breathing

### What Nasal Polyps Do NOT Typically Cause
- Pain (unless there is an accompanying acute sinus infection)
- Bleeding (unexplained nosebleeds from a nasal mass warrant urgent investigation to exclude tumour)
- Fever (unless concurrent acute sinusitis)

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Examination
An ENT specialist will examine the nasal passages with an otoscope or headlight. Small polyps deep in the nasal cavity may not be visible without endoscopy.

### Nasal Endoscopy
A thin flexible or rigid telescope is passed through the nasal passage under local anaesthetic. This allows direct visualisation of the entire nasal cavity, sinus openings, and any polyps — including those too far back to see on standard examination. Endoscopy is the definitive clinical diagnostic tool.

### CT Scan of the Sinuses
Provides detailed three-dimensional imaging of the sinuses, showing the extent of polyps and any associated sinus disease. Essential for surgical planning. CT scanning identifies anatomical variants and determines which sinuses are involved.

### Allergy Testing
A skin prick test or specific IgE blood test identifies allergic triggers contributing to polyp formation. Identifying and addressing allergy is important for long-term management.

### Biopsy
In cases where the diagnosis is uncertain — particularly with unilateral polyps or atypical appearances — a tissue biopsy is performed to exclude malignancy.

## Treatment Options

Treatment of nasal polyps is directed at reducing inflammation, shrinking or removing the polyps, and addressing underlying causes to prevent recurrence. Recurrence is common — long-term management is often required.

### Medical Treatment

**Intranasal corticosteroid sprays:**
The cornerstone of medical treatment. Sprays such as fluticasone, mometasone, or budesonide reduce mucosal inflammation and can shrink polyps over weeks to months. They must be used correctly — pointing away from the nasal septum and toward the outer wall of the nose — and require consistent long-term use to be effective.

**Oral corticosteroids:**
A short course of oral prednisolone can rapidly shrink polyps, providing immediate symptomatic relief. They are effective but carry side effects with prolonged or repeated use and are therefore used for short-term control or as a "medical polypectomy" before surgery.

**Saline nasal irrigation:**
Daily rinsing of the nasal passages with isotonic or hypertonic saline solution reduces mucus, removes allergens and irritants, and improves drug delivery to the nasal mucosa. Highly recommended as an adjunct to medical treatment.

**Antihistamines:**
Useful when allergic rhinitis is a significant contributing factor.

**Antibiotics:**
Used for concurrent bacterial sinusitis rather than polyps themselves. Long-term low-dose macrolide antibiotics (e.g., clarithromycin) have anti-inflammatory properties and are used in selected cases of CRS with polyps.

**Biologic agents:**
Newer injectable medications (e.g., dupilumab) target the specific inflammatory pathways driving eosinophilic polyp formation. Highly effective for severe, recurrent polyps in selected patients — though cost currently limits availability in many settings.

### Surgical Treatment: Functional Endoscopic Sinus Surgery (FESS)

When medical treatment fails to provide adequate relief or when polyps are very large, surgery is recommended.

**FESS** is performed entirely through the nostrils using an endoscope — no external cuts are made. The surgeon:
- Removes polyps under direct endoscopic vision
- Widens the natural sinus drainage pathways
- Removes thickened, diseased mucosa
- Corrects structural problems (e.g., deviated septum) that contribute to poor drainage

**Benefits of FESS:**
- Restores nasal breathing
- Improves or restores sense of smell in many patients
- Reduces sinus infections
- Improves delivery of topical treatments post-surgery

**Recovery:**
Most patients go home the same day or after one night. Nasal packing is used in some cases. There is typically nasal congestion and discharge for 2–4 weeks post-operatively, with full recovery in 4–6 weeks.

**Recurrence:**
Polyps recur in a significant proportion of patients — particularly those with severe eosinophilic disease or asthma. Long-term medical treatment with intranasal steroids and continued ENT follow-up is essential after surgery.

## Preventing Polyp Recurrence

- **Continue intranasal steroid sprays** long-term after surgery and medical treatment
- **Daily saline irrigation** — keeps the nasal passages clear and reduces inflammation
- **Manage underlying allergy** — allergen avoidance, antihistamines, and immunotherapy if appropriate
- **Control asthma** — good asthma management reduces nasal inflammation
- **Avoid aspirin and NSAIDs** if you have AERD
- **Attend regular ENT follow-up** — early detection of recurrence allows prompt treatment before polyps become large again

## When to See an ENT Specialist

Seek ENT evaluation if you have:
- Persistent nasal blockage not responding to decongestants or nasal sprays
- Noticeable loss of smell lasting more than a few weeks
- Recurrent sinus infections
- A visible mass in the nostril
- One-sided nasal obstruction or bleeding (requires urgent assessment)
