# Throat Pain and Swallowing Difficulty: Causes, Diagnosis, and Treatment

Throat pain and difficulty swallowing — medically termed dysphagia — are among the most common complaints that bring patients to ENT clinics. Almost everyone experiences a sore throat at some point, but when pain becomes severe, persists beyond a week, or is accompanied by difficulty swallowing, it warrants careful evaluation by a specialist. Understanding the range of causes helps you know when to manage at home and when to seek expert care.

## Understanding the Anatomy

The throat (pharynx) is a muscular tube that serves as a shared pathway for air and food. It connects the nasal and oral cavities to the larynx (voice box) and oesophagus below. Swallowing is a complex, coordinated process involving over 30 muscles working in precise sequence. Inflammation, infection, structural changes, or neurological problems at any point in this pathway can cause pain or disruption to this process.

## Common Causes of Throat Pain

### Pharyngitis (Sore Throat)

Pharyngitis is inflammation of the pharynx — the most common cause of throat pain.

**Viral pharyngitis** accounts for the majority of cases, caused by:
- Rhinovirus (common cold)
- Influenza viruses
- Adenovirus
- Epstein-Barr virus (glandular fever / infectious mononucleosis)

Symptoms include a raw, scratchy throat, mild fever, and runny nose. Most resolve within 7–10 days with supportive care.

**Bacterial pharyngitis** — most commonly caused by *Streptococcus pyogenes* (Group A Strep) — presents with:
- Sudden, severe throat pain
- High fever (above 38°C)
- Swollen, tender lymph nodes in the neck
- White or yellow patches on the tonsils
- Absence of cough (distinguishes it from viral causes)

Strep throat requires antibiotic treatment to prevent complications including rheumatic fever and kidney problems.

### [Tonsillitis](/blog/tonsillitis-causes-symptoms)

Tonsillitis is inflammation of the tonsils, two masses of lymphatic tissue at the back of the throat. It causes:
- Intense throat pain, especially on swallowing
- Enlarged, red tonsils — often with white patches of pus
- Fever and chills
- Difficulty opening the mouth fully (trismus) in severe cases

Recurrent tonsillitis (more than 5–7 episodes per year) may warrant tonsillectomy (surgical removal).

### [Peritonsillar Abscess](/blog/tonsillitis-causes-symptoms)

A peritonsillar abscess is a collection of pus that forms beside the tonsil — a serious complication of tonsillitis. It causes:
- Severe, one-sided throat pain
- A "hot potato" muffled voice
- Difficulty opening the mouth (trismus)
- Drooling due to inability to swallow
- Fever and feeling very unwell

This is an ENT emergency requiring drainage, often under local or general anaesthesia, plus intravenous antibiotics.

### [Laryngopharyngeal Reflux (LPR)](/blog/submandibular-lump-sensation)

Acid from the stomach can travel up the oesophagus and reach the throat, causing chronic irritation. Unlike typical acid reflux (heartburn), LPR often presents with:
- A persistent sensation of something stuck in the throat (globus)
- Throat clearing
- Hoarseness, especially in the morning
- Mild, chronic throat discomfort
- Chronic cough

It is frequently underdiagnosed and responds well to dietary changes, posture adjustments, and acid-suppressing medication.

### Epiglottitis

Epiglottitis is inflammation of the epiglottis — the small flap of tissue that covers the airway during swallowing. Though rare in vaccinated populations, it remains a life-threatening emergency.

Symptoms include:
- Rapidly worsening sore throat and pain on swallowing
- Drooling
- Stridor (a high-pitched sound when breathing)
- Sitting forward to breathe (the "tripod position")

**Seek emergency care immediately if epiglottitis is suspected.**

## Understanding Dysphagia (Difficulty Swallowing)

Dysphagia is defined as difficulty initiating swallowing or the sensation of food or liquid being stuck after swallowing. It is classified by location:

### Oropharyngeal Dysphagia
Difficulty initiating the swallow — food or liquid may spill into the airway, causing choking or coughing. Causes include:
- Inflammation and swelling from acute infection
- Neurological conditions (stroke, Parkinson's disease)
- Structural abnormalities (large tonsils, post-surgical changes)
- Muscle weakness

### Oesophageal Dysphagia
The sensation of food sticking in the chest or throat after swallowing has been initiated. Causes include:
- Oesophageal stricture (narrowing from reflux scarring)
- Motility disorders (the oesophagus fails to push food down normally)
- [Foreign body](/blog/foreign-bodies-uganda)
- Oesophageal cancer

## Red Flag Symptoms: Seek Urgent ENT Evaluation

The following symptoms require prompt specialist assessment as they may indicate a serious underlying condition:

- **Throat pain lasting more than 3 weeks** without an obvious cause
- **Painful swallowing that is progressively worsening**
- **Unintentional weight loss** associated with swallowing difficulty
- **Blood in saliva or phlegm**
- **[A lump in the neck](/blog/submandibular-lump-sensation)** that appears alongside throat symptoms
- **Hoarseness persisting beyond 3 weeks**
- **One-sided throat pain** (raises concern for peritonsillar abscess or, rarely, throat cancer)
- **Difficulty breathing or stridor**

These symptoms do not automatically indicate cancer, but they require investigation to rule it out. Early detection significantly improves outcomes.

## Diagnosis

An ENT specialist will take a detailed history and perform:

**Throat examination:** Assessing the tonsils, pharynx, and soft palate with a headlight and tongue depressor.

**Flexible nasopharyngolaryngoscopy:** A thin, flexible camera passed through the nose provides a complete view of the nasal passages, nasopharynx, and larynx — structures not visible with a standard examination. This is performed in the clinic with topical anaesthetic.

**Neck examination:** Palpating the lymph nodes and salivary glands for enlargement or tenderness.

**Investigations:** Throat swab for culture, full blood count, monospot test (if glandular fever is suspected), and CT or MRI imaging where a mass or abscess is suspected.

**Oesophagoscopy / barium swallow:** For oesophageal dysphagia, to visualise the oesophagus and assess its function.

## Treatment Approaches

### Conservative Management
- **Rest and hydration** — adequate fluid intake is essential during throat infections
- **Salt-water gargles** — can provide temporary pain relief
- **Analgesics** — paracetamol or ibuprofen to manage pain and fever
- **Throat lozenges** — provide local anaesthetic relief

### Medical Treatment
- **Antibiotics** — for confirmed or strongly suspected bacterial pharyngitis and tonsillitis
- **Corticosteroids** — a short course can rapidly reduce severe swelling in tonsillitis or glandular fever
- **Antacids and proton pump inhibitors** — for LPR-related symptoms
- **Antifungal treatment** — for oral thrush (candida), which can cause throat pain particularly in patients on inhaled steroids or antibiotics

### Surgical Treatment
- **Peritonsillar abscess drainage** — needle aspiration or incision and drainage
- **Tonsillectomy** — for recurrent tonsillitis or obstructive tonsil enlargement
- **Adenoidectomy** — when adenoids contribute to swallowing or breathing problems
- **Endoscopic procedures** — for oesophageal strictures, foreign body removal, or laryngeal pathology

## Prevention Tips

- Wash hands regularly to reduce viral and bacterial transmission
- Avoid sharing utensils or drinks during illness
- Stay up to date with vaccinations
- Manage acid reflux with dietary changes — avoid spicy foods, caffeine, alcohol, and eating within 2–3 hours of bedtime
- Avoid smoking and second-hand smoke exposure
- Stay hydrated — a moist throat is more resistant to infection
