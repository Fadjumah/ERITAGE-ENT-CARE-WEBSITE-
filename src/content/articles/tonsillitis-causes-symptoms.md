# Tonsillitis: Causes, Symptoms & When to Seek ENT Care

Tonsillitis is one of the most common throat conditions we treat at Eritage ENT Care. Whether you are a parent worried about your child's recurring sore throats or an adult experiencing painful swallowing, understanding tonsillitis helps you know when home care is enough — and when professional ENT evaluation is essential. For more information on our specific services, visit our [tonsillitis treatment page for Entebbe](/conditions/tonsillitis-entebbe).

## What Are the Tonsils?

The tonsils are two oval-shaped pads of lymphoid tissue positioned at the back of the throat, one on each side. They are part of the body's immune system and act as the first line of defence against bacteria and viruses entering through the mouth and nose. The tonsils produce antibodies and white blood cells to fight infection.

However, the tonsils can themselves become overwhelmed by infection — and in some people, particularly children, this happens repeatedly, leading to significant disruption to daily life.

There are different types of tonsillar tissue in the throat:
- **Palatine tonsils** — the tonsils visible when you open your mouth wide (what most people refer to as "the tonsils")
- **Adenoids (pharyngeal tonsils)** — located at the back of the nasal cavity, not directly visible
- **Lingual tonsils** — at the base of the tongue

When people refer to tonsillitis, they usually mean inflammation of the palatine tonsils.

## What Causes Tonsillitis?

Tonsillitis can be caused by both viruses and bacteria, with viral causes being more common.

### Viral Tonsillitis
Viruses responsible for tonsillitis include:
- **Adenovirus** — one of the most common causes
- **Rhinovirus** — the common cold virus
- **Influenza virus** — associated with a more severe illness
- **Epstein-Barr virus (EBV)** — causes infectious mononucleosis (glandular fever), a particularly debilitating form of tonsillitis with extreme fatigue and marked tonsil swelling
- **Herpes simplex virus** — less common; causes distinctive blistering

Viral tonsillitis is not treated with antibiotics — it requires supportive care and time.

### Bacterial Tonsillitis
**Group A Streptococcus** (*Streptococcus pyogenes*, or "Strep A") is the most important bacterial cause, responsible for 15–30% of cases in children and up to 10% in adults. It is medically significant because untreated streptococcal tonsillitis can lead to:
- **Rheumatic fever** — an inflammatory condition affecting the heart, joints, and nervous system
- **Rheumatic heart disease** — a leading cause of acquired heart disease in Uganda and the wider African region
- **Post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis** — kidney inflammation
- **Peritonsillar abscess** — a collection of pus beside the tonsil

This is why correctly identifying and treating streptococcal tonsillitis with antibiotics is important.

## Symptoms of Tonsillitis

Symptoms vary depending on the cause and severity, but typically include:

**Throat symptoms:**
- Sore throat — often described as one of the worst sore throats the patient has experienced
- Pain on swallowing (odynophagia), which may be severe enough to prevent eating and drinking
- Muffled or altered voice

**General symptoms:**
- Fever — often above 38°C in bacterial cases
- Headache
- Chills
- Fatigue and malaise
- Bad breath (halitosis) — from the pus and bacteria on the tonsils

**On examination:**
- Red, swollen tonsils
- White or yellow patches of exudate (pus) on the tonsils
- Swollen, tender lymph nodes (glands) in the neck
- Redness at the back of the throat

### Differences Between Viral and Bacterial Tonsillitis

| Feature | Viral | Bacterial (Strep) |
|---|---|---|
| Onset | Gradual | Sudden |
| Cough | Common | Usually absent |
| Runny nose | Common | Uncommon |
| Fever | Mild to moderate | High (>38.5°C) |
| Exudate (pus) | Less common | Common |
| Response to antibiotics | None | Yes |

The **Centor criteria** and modified versions (FeverPAIN) help clinicians estimate the likelihood of streptococcal infection to guide antibiotic prescribing decisions.

## Home Care for Mild Tonsillitis

Most cases of viral tonsillitis and mild bacterial tonsillitis with appropriate antibiotics can be managed at home:

- **Rest** — allow the body's immune system to do its work
- **Adequate fluids** — staying hydrated is essential, especially if a fever is present; cold drinks, ice lollies, and smoothies can be easier to swallow
- **Analgesics** — paracetamol (Panadol) or ibuprofen to manage pain and fever; ibuprofen has the added benefit of anti-inflammatory action
- **Salt-water gargles** — warm salt water provides temporary soothing relief for older children and adults
- **Soft foods** — avoid hard, scratchy foods that worsen throat discomfort; soups, yoghurt, and mashed foods are easier to swallow

**Important:** If bacterial tonsillitis (strep throat) is confirmed or strongly suspected, complete the full course of prescribed antibiotics even if symptoms improve after a few days. Stopping early risks relapse and does not allow enough time to eliminate the bacteria and prevent complications.

## Warning Signs: When to Seek Urgent Care

Go to an ENT specialist or emergency department if you or your child develops:

- **Difficulty breathing** or audible stridor (a harsh, high-pitched sound when breathing)
- **Drooling** or inability to swallow saliva
- **Inability to open the mouth** (trismus) — suggests a peritonsillar abscess
- **One-sided throat pain** that is rapidly worsening — also suggests abscess
- **Neck stiffness** with throat symptoms — must rule out deep neck infection
- **Symptoms that are rapidly worsening** despite antibiotic treatment
- **Dehydration** — no urine output for 8+ hours, very dry mouth, severe lethargy in a child

**Peritonsillar abscess** is the most common complication of tonsillitis. Pus collects in the space beside the tonsil, causing severe one-sided pain, a "hot potato" muffled voice, and difficulty opening the mouth. It requires drainage — usually by needle aspiration or incision — plus intravenous antibiotics, and ideally same-day ENT review.

## Recurrent Tonsillitis: When Is Tonsillectomy Considered?

Some people — particularly children between the ages of 3 and 15 — experience tonsillitis repeatedly. Recurrent tonsillitis:
- Disrupts school attendance and academic performance
- Leads to repeated courses of antibiotics
- Causes significant pain and discomfort across the family

### When ENT Specialists Recommend Tonsillectomy

The widely accepted **Paradise criteria** guide tonsillectomy recommendations:
- **7 or more** documented episodes in the preceding year, OR
- **5 or more** episodes per year for 2 consecutive years, OR
- **3 or more** episodes per year for 3 consecutive years

Additional indications include:
- Tonsils so large they obstruct breathing during sleep (obstructive sleep apnoea)
- A peritonsillar abscess that has recurred
- Suspicion of a tonsillar tumour (rare)

### What Tonsillectomy Involves

Tonsillectomy is one of the most commonly performed surgical procedures worldwide. It is done under general anaesthesia, typically takes 20–30 minutes, and is usually performed as a day case.

Recovery:
- **Days 1–3:** Most painful; throat and referred ear pain are common
- **Days 4–7:** The wound develops a white coating (not infection) as it heals
- **Days 7–10:** Risk of secondary bleeding — avoid strenuous activity
- **Full recovery:** Usually 2 weeks for children; adults may take slightly longer

Benefits in appropriately selected patients are significant and lasting — the majority of patients have dramatically fewer throat infections after tonsillectomy.

## Tonsillitis in Adults

Tonsillitis is often perceived as a childhood condition, but adults are also affected. Adults tend to recover more slowly from tonsillitis and are at higher risk of developing peritonsillar abscess. Recurrent tonsillitis in adults is equally amenable to tonsillectomy, and adults also experience significant quality-of-life improvement after surgery.

Adults over 45 with a new tonsil asymmetry (one tonsil noticeably larger than the other), particularly if associated with throat pain, difficulty swallowing, or a neck lump, require urgent ENT assessment to rule out tonsillar malignancy.

## Tonsillitis in the Ugandan Context

In Uganda and across sub-Saharan Africa, the link between streptococcal tonsillitis and rheumatic heart disease remains clinically important. Rheumatic fever following untreated strep throat can cause lifelong heart valve damage. Ensuring that bacterial tonsillitis is properly treated with a full course of antibiotics — and that recurrent streptococcal infections are addressed, including by tonsillectomy where indicated — is a meaningful contribution to preventing this preventable disease.
